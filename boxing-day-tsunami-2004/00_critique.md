## LINE-LEVEL CRITIQUE

### You-As-Subject — 5/10

**Failing line (Clip 03):** "There's no warning system. No sirens. The wave crosses open ocean in hours, but nobody on shore knows it's coming until they see it."
**Problem:** "You" does not appear once in this clip. It reads as a documentary narrator describing the situation from a god's-eye view. "Nobody on shore" is third-person. The viewer is removed from the experience at exactly the moment they should be getting trapped in it.
**Fix:** "You have no warning system. No sirens. The wave crosses open ocean in hours — and you have no idea it's coming until you see it."

**Failing line (Clip 05):** "Two hundred and thirty thousand people died across fourteen countries. The ocean warned them — they just didn't know what it meant."
**Problem:** "People" and "them" — the viewer has been ejected from the story at the final beat. The shift to third-person in the closing destroys the "you-as-subject" contract the series depends on.
**Fix:** "Two hundred and thirty thousand people died across fourteen countries. You watched the ocean pull back. You didn't know what it meant."

### Narrator Restraint — 5/10

**Failing line (Clip 01):** "People walk out to look."
**Problem:** This is subtle but it's doing foreshadowing work — the narrator is telling us people did something naive, setting up dramatic irony. The narrator knows what's coming and is staging the victims as oblivious. The viewer feels the hand of the storyteller guiding them to think "oh no, those poor fools."
**Fix:** "The ocean pulls back hundreds of meters from the beach. You walk out onto the exposed seafloor." — Now it's just a thing "you" do. No judgment, no omniscient staging of other people's ignorance.

**Failing line (Clip 03):** "but nobody on shore knows it's coming until they see it."
**Problem:** "Until they see it" is a textbook foreshadowing beat. The narrator is building suspense by telling us the characters don't know what we know. This is dramatic irony, not restraint. The narrator is effectively saying "and then the bad thing arrives."
**Fix:** "You have no warning system. No sirens. The wave has been crossing open ocean for hours. You're still standing on the beach." — Factual. The narrator states where you are. The dread comes from the viewer's own understanding, not the narrator's wink.

**Failing line (Clip 04):** "You can't outrun it."
**Problem:** The narrator is commenting on your fate. This is editorial — it tells the viewer how to feel about the situation rather than describing what happens. It's the narrator stepping in to underline the horror.
**Fix:** "The water doesn't crash and retreat. It rises and keeps coming — three stories high, carrying cars, boats, buildings. You run." — Shorter, more brutal. The insufficiency of running is obvious without the narrator spelling it out.

### Understanding — 6/10

**Failing line (Clip 02):** "It displaced enough water to fill every swimming pool on earth — and pushed it toward you at the speed of a jet."
**Problem:** This is vivid but it's scale-painting, not mechanism-building. The viewer learns "big earthquake, lots of water, fast." But they don't learn WHY the coast was so vulnerable. The script's most important explanatory beat — no warning system existed for the Indian Ocean — gets buried as a throwaway in Clip 03 instead of being built into the causal chain. The viewer can say "big wave, no warning, lots of people died" but can't explain *why* there was no warning, or why communities were caught so flatfooted compared to, say, Japan.
**Fix:** Trim the swimming pool comparison and use those words to explain the gap: "A magnitude 9.1 earthquake just ruptured the seafloor — displacing a wall of water moving toward you at the speed of a jet. The Pacific has tsunami sirens. The Indian Ocean has none."
