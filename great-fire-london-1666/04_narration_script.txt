What happens if you find yourself in history's most famous inferno? You wake up in Thomas Farriner's bakery. The bread ovens glow with banked coals. It's two in the morning, September second, sixteen sixty-six. Sparks drift from the ovens. This is not good. You stumble into Pudding Lane as timber buildings catch fire. Neighbors flee in their nightshirts. London hasn't seen rain in months. The flames spread faster than you can run. You join thousands fleeing toward London Bridge. The medieval city burns behind you in a crescent of orange fire. Surely stone is safer than wood. But the bridge gatehouse becomes a trap. The portcullis jammed shut decades ago. Medieval crowd control fails completely. You realize safety is always an illusion. You're trampled onto the bridge deck as burning debris rains down. The bridge itself catches fire. Massive timber beams glow red-hot. You slide toward the Thames forty feet below, still reaching for help that never comes. Four days later, they find you in the Thames mud. Exactly where you fell. Still reaching upward. The Great Fire consumed eighty-seven churches and thirteen thousand homes. But it preserved you perfectly.
