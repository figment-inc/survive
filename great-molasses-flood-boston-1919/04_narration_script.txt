What happens if two million gallons of molasses chase you down a street? You wake up on cobblestones. The air is thick and sweet. It's January in Boston, and something is very wrong. That steel tank above you holds two million gallons. It's been leaking for years. You press your hand to the metal. It groans. It flexes. It's alive with pressure. You try to warn them. The longshoremen laugh. The children keep playing. A grandmother walks right past you. Only the horse understands. It pulls at its rope, nostrils flared. At twelve-thirty, the rivets pop like gunshots. The tank splits open. A wave of molasses fifteen feet high surges at thirty-five miles per hour. You run. It's faster. It swallows the railway, the buildings, the street. Twenty-one people will not go home tonight. You duck into an alley. The molasses follows. It rises to your waist. You jump for a fire escape. You miss. Here's the thing about molasses — the colder it gets, the harder it holds. The January air does its work. The flood thickens. You cannot move your legs. Sailors from the USS Nantucket wade in wearing white. They leave wearing brown. One hundred fifty people are trapped. Rescue takes four days. The smell will linger for decades. You lie at the flood's edge. A doorway, ten feet ahead, stands open. Your hand reaches for it. You never arrive. For thirty years, on hot summer days, the North End smells of molasses. A sweet ghost. Just like you.
