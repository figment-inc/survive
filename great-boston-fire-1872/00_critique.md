## LINE-LEVEL CRITIQUE

### Sensory Immersion — 4/10

**Failing line (Clip 02):** "You work in the dry goods district — sixty acres of granite warehouses packed with wool, leather, and paper."
**Problem:** This is an inventory list. Wool, leather, and paper are nouns, not sensations. The viewer should smell this district — lanolin, tanning chemicals, dust. These materials matter because they burn; the script should make the viewer feel how combustible this air is.
**Fix:** "You work in the dry goods district — sixty acres of granite warehouses. The air is thick with wool fiber and paper dust. It coats your tongue."

**Failing line (Clip 06):** "You watch the fire jump the street."
**Problem:** "You watch" is the enemy of immersion. The viewer is an observer, not a body in the scene. Where is the heat on the face? The sound? The smoke?
**Fix:** "The heat pushes you back from the curb. The fire jumps the street."

**Failing line (Clip 07):** "Burning timber arcs across intersections."
**Problem:** This is cinematic — it's a camera shot, not a bodily experience. The viewer sees a wide shot of timber flying. They should feel embers landing on their coat, hear the crack of joists giving way overhead.
**Fix:** "Embers land on your shoulders. Burning timber arcs across the intersection ahead of you."

**Failing line (Clip 08):** "Granite walls hold until the heat cracks them from within. Entire facades shear off and collapse into the street, burying the hose lines beneath them."
**Problem:** This is the peak catastrophe clip and there is zero bodily sensation. The viewer is watching a nature documentary about granite. Where is the concussive blast of a wall falling? The ground shaking under your feet? The dust choking your throat?
**Fix:** "The granite pops and splits — you feel it through your boots. Entire facades shear off. The street shakes. Dust blinds you. The hose lines vanish under rubble."

**Failing line (Clip 10):** "You stand in a landscape that no longer has streets."
**Problem:** This is a strong image but still visual-only. What does aftermath feel like? Heat radiating from rubble. Ash underfoot. The silence after twelve hours of roar.
**Fix:** "You stand on rubble still hot enough to burn through your soles. The streets are gone."

### You-As-Subject — 5/10

**Failing line (Clip 04):** "A month before the fire, an equine epidemic sweeps the eastern seaboard. The Great Epizootic. Every draft horse in Boston falls ill."
**Problem:** Three consecutive sentences with no "you." This is a history lecture. The viewer has left their body and is reading a textbook.
**Fix:** "A month before the fire, you notice the horses coughing. The Great Epizootic. Every draft horse in Boston falls ill. You see empty harnesses hanging in the engine houses."

**Failing line (Clip 08):** "Granite walls hold until the heat cracks them from within. Entire facades shear off and collapse into the street, burying the hose lines beneath them."
**Problem:** Peak catastrophe, and "you" is entirely absent. The buildings are the subject of every clause. This is the moment the viewer should be most physically present and they have been removed from the scene entirely.
**Fix:** "You hear the granite crack before you see it move. The facade shears off. You run. The hose lines vanish under stone."

**Failing line (Clip 09):** "The chief orders buildings dynamited to create a firebreak. The demolition men arrive. They have no experience. The explosions spread the fire further."
**Problem:** Four sentences, zero "you." The chief acts. The men arrive. The explosions spread. The viewer is watching from across the harbor.
**Fix:** "The chief orders buildings dynamited to create a firebreak. You feel the blast in your chest. The demolition men have no experience. The explosions spread the fire further."

**Failing line (Clip 11):** "Seven hundred and seventy-six buildings gone. The damage, in today's currency, exceeds four billion dollars. The engines were never late because they were broken."
**Problem:** Statistics clip with no "you" — acceptable for The Number beat, but "The engines were never late because they were broken" is the setup for the callback and it reads as a third-person history conclusion, not something felt.
**Fix:** "Seven hundred and seventy-six buildings gone. Everything you could see from your warehouse door. The engines were never late because they were broken."

### Rhythm Shift — 5/10

**Failing line (Clip 07):** "Mansard roofs — fashionable, wooden, tarred — catch like matchheads. Burning timber arcs across intersections. You retreat one block. Then another. Then another."
**Problem:** The last three fragments are excellent staccato. But the first sentence is 10 words plus a parenthetical clause — it reads like a setup clip, not catastrophe. The rhythm shift arrives halfway through the clip instead of hitting immediately.
**Fix:** "Mansard roofs catch like matchheads. Wooden. Tarred. Burning timber arcs overhead. You retreat one block. Then another. Then another."

**Failing line (Clip 08):** "Granite walls hold until the heat cracks them from within. Entire facades shear off and collapse into the street, burying the hose lines beneath them."
**Problem:** Two long, complex sentences (12 and 15 words). This is the second catastrophe clip and it reads with the same flowing rhythm as Clip 03. There is no acceleration. The viewer should feel sentences shattering like the granite.
**Fix:** "The granite cracks. Walls split from within. Facades shear off. They collapse into the street. The hose lines disappear."

**Failing line (Clip 09):** "The chief orders buildings dynamited to create a firebreak. The demolition men arrive. They have no experience. The explosions spread the fire further."
**Problem:** This clip is post-catastrophe and should maintain the staccato energy. The first sentence is 9 words — borderline acceptable — but "to create a firebreak" is an explanatory dependent clause that slows the rhythm. The last two sentences are good.
**Fix:** "The chief orders buildings dynamited. A firebreak. The demolition men have no experience. The explosions spread the fire further."
