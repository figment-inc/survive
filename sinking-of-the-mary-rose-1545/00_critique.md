## LINE-LEVEL CRITIQUE

### Sensory Immersion — 5/10

**Failing line (Clip 06):** "— and the Channel pours in. Not a wave. Not a storm. Just a turn taken with the ports left wide. The water reaches the gun deck in seconds."

**Problem:** This is narrated observation. Where is the sensation of cold seawater hitting your legs? The weight of it? The sound? The viewer should feel the Channel, not be told it "pours in." "Reaches the gun deck in seconds" is a timeline, not a sensation.

**Fix:** "— and the Channel pours in. The water hits your shins — July, and it is cold enough to lock your breath. It reaches your waist before you turn around."

---

**Failing line (Clip 07):** "You scramble for the hatch, but anti-boarding netting is lashed across the deck. It was strung to keep the French out. Now it keeps you in."

**Problem:** "Scramble" is the only physical verb, and it's generic. What does being tangled in netting feel like? What are your hands doing? The irony is doing the work here, not sensation.

**Fix:** "You claw for the hatch. Your fingers catch rope netting — anti-boarding mesh, lashed tight across the deck. You pull, but the knots hold. They were tied to keep the French out."

---

**Failing line (Clip 08):** "Cannons break loose and slide across the deck. Men tangled in the netting go under with the hull still above them."

**Problem:** We're watching from outside. What does a two-ton bronze cannon sliding toward you sound like? Feel like? The deck is tilting — what does that do to your body?

**Fix:** "The deck tilts past standing. You hang from the netting. A demi-cannon — two tons of bronze — tears free of its carriage and grinds across the planking toward you."

---

### You-As-Subject — 6/10

**Failing line (Clip 08):** "Men tangled in the netting go under with the hull still above them."

**Problem:** "Men" is not "you." This is the climax of the catastrophe and the viewer just became a bystander watching other people die. Unacceptable at the peak of the script.

**Fix:** "You are tangled in the netting. The hull is still above you when the water takes your head."

---

**Failing line (Clip 09):** "On shore, Henry watches. He hears the wife of the admiral cry out. He says nothing. There is nothing to say."

**Problem:** I understand the dramatic purpose — the king's impotence mirrors the hook. But "you" has completely vanished for an entire clip. The viewer floats out of their body and into a third-person history documentary. This is the Human Gesture clip, but it should gesture at YOUR humanity.

**Fix:** "You are already under. On shore, Henry hears the wife of the admiral cry out. He says nothing." — One short sentence anchors "you" before cutting to the king.

---

**Failing line (Clip 10):** "The masts remain above the surface, flags still flying. Beneath them, five hundred men are trapped inside a ship that sank in less than a minute."

**Problem:** Two consecutive sentences, neither with "you" as subject. "Five hundred men" is a statistic. The viewer was one of those men thirty seconds ago.

**Fix:** "The masts remain above the surface, flags still flying. You are beneath them. Five hundred of you, inside a ship that sank in less than a minute."

---

### Rhythm Shift — 6/10

**Failing line (Clip 07):** "You scramble for the hatch, but anti-boarding netting is lashed across the deck. It was strung to keep the French out. Now it keeps you in."

**Problem:** The first sentence is 16 words. The second is 11. This is flowing explanatory prose — appropriate for clip 04, not for catastrophe. The last sentence ("Now it keeps you in") is perfect staccato, but it's buried after two longer setups. The clip needs to feel like panic, not exposition.

**Fix:** "You scramble for the hatch. Rope netting — lashed tight. Anti-boarding mesh. It was strung to keep the French out. Now it keeps you in."

---

**Failing line (Clip 08):** "The ship rolls past forty degrees. Cannons break loose and slide across the deck. Men tangled in the netting go under with the hull still above them."

**Problem:** All three sentences are 8–14 words with similar cadence. No acceleration. Compare to the gold standard: "Your body consumes itself. Muscle first. Then organ fat." The catastrophe should sound like the ship is capsizing — short, percussive, no time to breathe.

**Fix:** "Forty degrees. The cannons tear loose. Bronze slides across the deck. The hull rolls over you. The netting holds you under."
