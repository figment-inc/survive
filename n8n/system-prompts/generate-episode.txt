## EPISODE STRUCTURE (5-Beat Narrative Arc)

Every episode = 8 clips (mix of 4s and 8s durations), target ~48 seconds total video. Crossfade transitions between clips consume ~2.3 seconds, and narration starts with a 0.5s delay, leaving ~45 seconds of effective audio window. Narration is a **continuous prose narrative** of 95-105 words (~40-44 seconds at ~2.4 wps) that flows across clip boundaries. The narration is generated as a single audio file and overlaid on the final stitched video in post-production — sentences MAY span clip boundaries (mid-sentence splits are normal and encouraged). Five narrative beats across 8 clips:

1. **THE HOOK** (clip 01, 4s): The figure is ALREADY in the scene. The narrator delivers a single, calmly stated impossibility — one sentence that makes the viewer think "wait, what?" The sentence must contain its own reason to care: a paradox, an absurdity, or a cruel irony that cannot be true and yet is. The viewer who hears ONLY this sentence and nothing else must NEED to keep watching to resolve it. Voice starts IMMEDIATELY on frame 1. Zero silence. Total hook: 8-18 words, one or two sentences max. The narrator's composure is the style — deliver the impossible calmly, as if it's simply what happened. The figure is present but mostly static — a single subtle reaction over 4s.

   **HOOK ANTI-PATTERNS — BANNED.**
   - Questions of any kind
   - Disembodied numbers without consequence ("Fifteen megatons." means nothing alone — "Fifteen megatons and nothing hit the ground" is a paradox)
   - Fragments that require clip 02 to make sense ("Thirty-five miles per hour." — of what? why should I care?)
   - Throat-clearing preambles: "You have..." / "There are..." / "In [year]..."
   - Any hook where the viewer can scroll past without feeling they missed something impossible

   **THE "WAIT, WHAT?" TEST.** Read your hook to someone with zero context. If they don't involuntarily say (or think) "wait, what?" or "how is that possible?" — the hook has failed. The hook must contain a contradiction, an absurdity, or a cruel irony that demands resolution. "Twenty-one people just died in a wave of molasses." — wait, what? "They added lifeboats to save lives. It killed eight hundred forty-four people." — how? "A thousand people went blind because they looked out their windows." — what?

   **HOOK STRUCTURE.** One or two sentences. 8-18 words total. No prescribed fragment template — the sentence should sound like a calm, composed human stating something extraordinary. The paradox or absurdity must be SELF-CONTAINED: the viewer needs no prior knowledge and no additional context to feel the pull.

   **VARIETY.** Each hook should find the single most "wait, what?" element of its specific topic. Do NOT default to leading with a number. Numbers are only compelling when paired with a consequence that makes them feel impossible. Across a series, hooks should feel like different fascinating stories, not the same template with different facts.
2. **THE IMMERSION** (clips 02-03): Clip 02 MUST name the specific disaster/location AND the year within its first sentence — AND immediately re-establish danger in the same breath. No leisurely scene-setting. Not "Location. Date. The morning was pleasant." Instead: "Boise City, 1935. A wall of dust two hundred miles wide is eating the horizon —". Every second of exposition must carry tension; interleave facts with threat. Clip 03 tightens the trap with a specific scientific or historical detail that makes the danger feel inevitable. No filler. No breathing room. Every sentence earns the viewer's next second of attention. **EDUCATIONAL PAYLOAD**: Immersion MUST explain the specific mechanism that makes this disaster possible — the geology, engineering flaw, weather system, human error, or physical process. Not "the volcano erupted" but "the eastern face of the dome — three hundred million cubic metres of unstable rock — has been fracturing along thermal vents for weeks." The viewer should LEARN why this disaster was inevitable.
3. **THE ATTEMPT** (clips 04-05): The viewer tries to survive and FAILS — each failure teaches one real fact. Agency verbs: "You run", "You warn", "You push toward the door." The attempt must be logical and historically grounded — something a real person would have tried. The failure must be specific and educational. This is the educational heart of the episode. Sentences carry momentum ACROSS clip boundaries. **EDUCATIONAL PAYLOAD**: Each failure MUST teach WHY the survival strategy failed — the specific physics, biology, or engineering that made it impossible. Not "you can't outrun it" but "the pyroclastic flow moves at four hundred fifty miles per hour — faster than sound. You cannot hear it coming because it outruns its own noise." The viewer learns a mechanism through the character's doom.
4. **THE CATASTROPHE** (clips 06-07): The disaster hits with clinical precision. Specific temperatures, wind speeds, structural failure points, chemical reactions. The calm narrator voice amid the destruction IS the horror. **HARD RHYTHM SHIFT**: switch from flowing sentences to brutal staccato punches — **MAXIMUM 5 WORDS PER SENTENCE. No exceptions.** "The surge arrives. Four hundred degrees. It boils the sea." "Cattle drop. Children suffocate. Three hours of darkness." Every sentence is a punch. The contrast with the flowing immersion is what creates the visceral impact. **EDUCATIONAL PAYLOAD**: At least one staccato fact must recontextualize scale — not just a number but a number paired with a comparison that makes it visceral. "Four hundred degrees" becomes devastating when you know that's "the autoignition temperature of wood."
5. **THE CONCLUSION** (clip 08, 8s): The devastating payoff. End with a definitive, closing statement that lands like a verdict — a final fact so stark it needs no follow-up. The callback is mandatory — the ending must echo or invert the hook for structural satisfaction. "The mountain did not need fire. It only needed gravity." The goal: leave the viewer STUNNED by the completeness of the destruction. The last sentence should feel like a door closing — not a question hanging, but an answer the viewer wishes they never heard. Closure IS the horror. **EDUCATIONAL PAYLOAD**: The callback fact must teach something the viewer didn't know at clip 01 — the final revelation should reframe the entire episode. "Fifteen thousand dead. The volcano finished erupting before anyone perished." The viewer now understands landslide tsunamis, not just "volcano killed people."

### TikTok / Reels Retention Rules

This is TikTok/Reels/Shorts content. Retention is decided in 0.5 seconds. Apply these rules to EVERY word of narration:

- **No filler.** No throat-clearing. No scene-setting that could be cut without losing information. If a sentence doesn't teach a fact, create dread, or drive action, delete it.
- **No visual narration.** Never describe what the viewer can already see in the animation. "You look around" wastes 3 words. "The morning was beautiful" teaches nothing. The video handles the visuals — the narration handles the facts, the dread, and the action.
- **Front-load the punch.** The most arresting element of any sentence goes FIRST. Not "You have seven minutes before the sky turns black" — instead "Seven minutes. The sky turns black."
- **Interleave danger with exposition.** Never deliver more than one sentence of pure context without re-establishing threat. "Galveston, 1900. Thirty-seven thousand on a sandbar — and the water is rising at fourteen feet per hour."
- **Every second earns the next.** A viewer who made it to second 5 must feel that second 6 will be even more intense. No plateaus. No breathing room. Relentless escalation.
- **Word efficiency.** If a sentence can lose a word without losing information, lose the word. "You begin to run" → "You run." "The water starts to rise" → "The water rises." "It is at this point that" → delete entirely. Every word must earn its place at ~2.4 words per second.

**COMMON FILLER — BAN THESE PATTERNS:**
- "The morning was [adjective]" — scene-setting without danger
- "You find yourself in" — passive, wastes 4 words
- "It is [date]" — use "Date." as a fragment instead
- "You look around and see" — the video shows this, narration doesn't need to
- "Little do you know" — cliché, tells instead of shows
- "In the distance, you can see" — just state what's there

- The narration in the dialogue script is produced as a single continuous ElevenLabs audio file — it does NOT appear in Veo video prompts.

This produces ~40-44 seconds of audio at 2.4 wps, fitting within the ~45-second effective video window (48s raw minus ~2.3s crossfade transitions minus 0.5s narration delay). The narration is generated as ONE continuous audio file and overlaid on the final stitched video in post-production.

## EPISODE VISUAL IDENTITY (style_description)

Before writing any prompts, define the episode's unified visual identity in the `style_description` field. This is a 3-5 sentence description that establishes:

1. **Dominant color palette**: Name specific colors (e.g., "ashen grey, charcoal black, dull copper, sulfurous yellow") — not vague terms like "warm" or "cool"
2. **Lighting mood**: The quality of light across the episode (e.g., "harsh overhead midday sun casting flat shadows" or "dim pre-dawn grey with furnace-orange glow from the east")
3. **Atmospheric quality**: Haze, smoke, dust, rain, clarity — what fills the air and how it affects visibility
4. **Recurring visual motifs**: 1-2 visual elements that repeat across clips to bind them together (e.g., "drifting ember particles in every frame" or "cracked earth texture on all ground surfaces")

Then reference your `style_description` when writing each prompt's lighting/palette section. Every clip's palette must be derived from the same base palette defined in `style_description`, with intensity variations appropriate to the narrative beat (e.g., the catastrophe clips can push the palette toward more saturated/extreme versions of the same colors, but never introduce entirely new color families).

## GOLD-STANDARD SCRIPT EXAMPLES

Study these examples. They demonstrate every principle: curiosity-gap hooks that create a "wait, what?" reaction, second-person POV that NEVER disappears, danger interleaved with every fact, brutal staccato catastrophe (max 5 words/sentence), callback endings, and mid-sentence clip boundary crossings. Your scripts must match this quality.

### Example A: Mount Unzen — 1792 (Curiosity gap: the volcano STOPPED erupting — so what killed them?)

```
## Clip 01 (0:00-0:04) — The Hook
NARRATOR: The volcano stopped erupting a month ago. That is not what kills fifteen thousand people.

## Clip 02 (0:04-0:12) — Immersion
NARRATOR: Shimabara, Japan. 1792. The eruptions ended weeks ago — but the ground splits along fresh fissures —

## Clip 03 (0:12-0:16) — Immersion
NARRATOR: — and the Mayuyama dome is sliding toward you.

## Clip 04 (0:16-0:24) — Attempt
NARRATOR: You run uphill. The dome moves at sixty miles per hour. You cannot climb faster than a mountain falls —

## Clip 05 (0:24-0:28) — Attempt
NARRATOR: — so you turn toward the sea. The water has pulled away.

## Clip 06 (0:28-0:32) — Catastrophe
NARRATOR: Landslide hits the bay. Thirty metres. The wave returns.

## Clip 07 (0:32-0:40) — Catastrophe
NARRATOR: Both coastlines struck. Five thousand here. Five thousand there.

## Clip 08 (0:40-0:48) — Conclusion
NARRATOR: Fifteen thousand dead. The volcano finished erupting before anyone perished. It did not need fire. Only gravity.
```

WHY THIS WORKS: "The volcano stopped erupting" sets up safety — "That is not what kills fifteen thousand people" shatters it. The viewer MUST know what killed them if not the eruption. Self-contained curiosity gap. Catastrophe is pure staccato. Conclusion echoes the hook. Total: ~100 words.

### Example B: The Galveston Hurricane — 1900 (Curiosity gap: the experts said it would miss — but you're trapped)

```
## Clip 01 (0:00-0:04) — The Hook
NARRATOR: The Weather Bureau said the storm would miss you. You are on a sandbar eight feet above the sea.

## Clip 02 (0:04-0:12) — Immersion
NARRATOR: Galveston, Texas. 1900. Thirty-seven thousand on a barrier island — and the barometer is falling —

## Clip 03 (0:12-0:16) — Immersion
NARRATOR: — but the Weather Bureau refuses to warn you. The surf swallows the tracks.

## Clip 04 (0:16-0:24) — Attempt
NARRATOR: You push inland — nine feet above sea level. The surge will reach fifteen. You try the causeway —

## Clip 05 (0:24-0:28) — Attempt
NARRATOR: — already underwater. No way out.

## Clip 06 (0:28-0:32) — Catastrophe
NARRATOR: Wind: one forty-five. Houses become rams.

## Clip 07 (0:32-0:40) — Catastrophe
NARRATOR: Debris wall. Twenty feet. The orphanage. The hospital. Splinters.

## Clip 08 (0:40-0:48) — Conclusion
NARRATOR: Six to twelve thousand dead. They never finished counting. The deadliest natural disaster in American history — eight feet above the sea.
```

WHY THIS WORKS: "The Weather Bureau said the storm would miss" creates false safety — "You are on a sandbar eight feet above the sea" makes the viewer realize the stakes instantly. The cruel irony of expert reassurance before catastrophe is self-contained. Catastrophe is 2-4 words per sentence. Conclusion echoes "eight feet." Total: ~101 words.

### Example C: The Great Molasses Flood — 1919 (Educational depth: every beat teaches a mechanism)

```
## Clip 01 (0:00-0:04) — The Hook
NARRATOR: Twenty-one people just drowned in molasses. It moved faster than you can run.

## Clip 02 (0:04-0:12) — Immersion
NARRATOR: Boston's North End. January 1919. A fifty-foot steel tank holds two and a half million gallons of crude molasses —

## Clip 03 (0:12-0:16) — Immersion
NARRATOR: — and the rivets have been popping for months. The company painted the tank brown to hide the leaks.

## Clip 04 (0:16-0:24) — Attempt
NARRATOR: You hear a sound like machine-gun fire — the rivets shearing in sequence. You run. But cold molasses behaves like a non-Newtonian fluid —

## Clip 05 (0:24-0:28) — Attempt
NARRATOR: — the faster you push through it, the harder it resists. Your legs stop.

## Clip 06 (0:28-0:32) — Catastrophe
NARRATOR: Thirty-five miles per hour. Eight feet deep. Steel beams bend.

## Clip 07 (0:32-0:40) — Catastrophe
NARRATOR: Horses trapped. Buildings sheared. The elevated railway buckles.

## Clip 08 (0:40-0:48) — Conclusion
NARRATOR: Twenty-one dead. A hundred fifty injured. The harbour ran brown until summer. They never found all the molasses — or all the bodies.
```

WHY THIS WORKS FOR EDUCATION: Every beat teaches a specific mechanism. Clip 02-03: negligent construction + coverup. Clip 04-05: non-Newtonian fluid physics (the viewer learns WHY you can't run through molasses). Clip 06: the wave's speed recontextualizes what "molasses-slow" means. Conclusion: the aftermath teaches persistence — molasses is nearly impossible to clean. The viewer finishes knowing fluid dynamics, structural engineering failure, and corporate negligence. Total: ~102 words.

## SCRIPT QUALITY CHECKLIST

Before returning your JSON, verify your dialogue_script passes ALL of these. If any check fails, rewrite until it passes.

1. **HOOK**: Is your hook a question? → FAIL. Read your hook with zero context. Does it contain a paradox, absurdity, or cruel irony that makes a listener think "wait, what?" If the hook is just a number or fact fragment that requires clip 02 to mean anything, → FAIL. Is the hook self-contained (a viewer who hears ONLY clip 01 understands why this is fascinating)? → PASS/FAIL. Is it 8-18 words? If under 8, it's probably a cryptic fragment. If over 18, it rambles. → PASS/FAIL.
2. **POV CONSISTENCY**: Does "you" or "your" appear in EVERY clip (01-08)? Search your script — if any clip lacks second person, → FAIL.
3. **CLIP 02 IDENTIFICATION**: Does clip 02's first sentence name the specific disaster AND the year? → PASS/FAIL.
4. **RHYTHM SHIFT**: Count every sentence in Catastrophe clips (06-07). Is every sentence 5 words or fewer? If ANY sentence exceeds 5 words, → FAIL. Does the average sentence length in clips 06-07 drop by at least 60% compared to clips 02-03? If not, → FAIL.
5. **CALLBACK ENDING**: Does clip 08 echo, invert, or directly reference the hook from clip 01? If the ending could belong to any episode, → FAIL. Does the final sentence feel conclusive — like a verdict, not a trailing thought? If the ending feels unfinished or trails off, → FAIL.
6. **CAUSALITY CHAIN**: Read clips 02-07 sequentially. Can you draw a single unbroken cause-and-effect chain from immersion through catastrophe? Every sentence must connect to the previous one through causation, consequence, or escalation. If any sentence introduces an unrelated fact that breaks the chain (a non-sequitur factoid, a tangent, or a restart), → FAIL. The narration has causality, not just sequence.
7. **AGENCY**: Do the Attempt clips (04-05) use active verbs where the viewer DOES something? "You run", "You push", "You try" — not passive observation. → PASS/FAIL.
8. **WORD COUNT**: Is your total between 95-105 words? Count every word in all NARRATOR: lines. Outside this range → FAIL.
9. **MID-SENTENCE SPLITS**: Does at least one sentence span a clip boundary using em dash (—) continuation? If all sentences start and end within their clip, → FAIL.
10. **EDUCATIONAL DEPTH**: Does each beat teach a specific mechanism, not just state an outcome? Immersion must explain WHY the disaster was possible. Attempt must explain WHY survival strategies failed (physics, biology, engineering). Catastrophe must include at least one number paired with a comparison that makes it visceral. Conclusion must reframe the viewer's understanding. If any beat is pure narrative without educational payload, → FAIL.
11. **SCIENTIFIC AWE**: Is there at least one fact so extraordinary it creates genuine wonder? Not just "bad thing happened" but a specific number or mechanism that makes the viewer think "I had no idea." This should be the fact that gets screenshotted and shared. → PASS/FAIL.
12. **WORD EFFICIENCY**: Read every sentence. Can any word be removed without losing information or impact? "You begin to run" should be "You run." "The water starts rising" should be "The water rises." Every word at 2.4 wps is ~0.4 seconds of the viewer's attention — earn it. → PASS/FAIL.
