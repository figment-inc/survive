## EPISODE STRUCTURE (5-Beat Narrative Arc)

Every episode = 8 clips (mix of 4s and 8s durations), target ~48 seconds total video. Crossfade transitions between clips consume ~2.3 seconds, and narration starts with a 0.5s delay, leaving ~45 seconds of effective audio window. Narration is a **continuous prose narrative** of 95-105 words (~40-44 seconds at ~2.4 wps) that flows across clip boundaries. The narration is generated as a single audio file and overlaid on the final stitched video in post-production — sentences MAY span clip boundaries (mid-sentence splits are normal and encouraged). Five narrative beats across 8 clips:

1. **THE HOOK + FRAMING LINE** (clip 01, 4s): Two parts, both in clip 01. Voice starts IMMEDIATELY on frame 1. Zero silence. The figure is ALREADY in the scene, present but mostly static — a single subtle reaction over 4s.

   **PART A — THE HOOK (8-18 words).** The narrator delivers a single, calmly stated impossibility — one or two sentences that make the viewer think "wait, what?" The sentence must contain its own reason to care: a paradox, an absurdity, or a cruel irony that cannot be true and yet is. The viewer who hears ONLY this sentence and nothing else must NEED to keep watching to resolve it. The narrator's composure is the style — deliver the impossible calmly, as if it's simply what happened.

   **PART B — THE FRAMING LINE (~8-12 words).** Immediately after the hook, the narrator says: **"You would not want to be in the [Event Name], [Year]."** This is MANDATORY in every episode. It tells the viewer exactly what this video is about. The framing line is narrated only — it is NOT rendered as on-screen text. Examples:
   - "You would not want to be in the Wall Street Crash of 1929."
   - "You would not want to be in the Great Molasses Flood of 1919."
   - "You would not want to be at Mount Unzen in 1792."
   - "You would not want to be in the Halifax Explosion of 1917."
   The phrasing can vary slightly ("in the...", "at...", "on the...") to sound natural for the specific event, but must always start with "You would not want to be" and must name the event and year.

   **HOOK + FRAMING LINE COMBINED: 18-28 words total for clip 01.**

   **HOOK ANTI-PATTERNS — BANNED.**
   - Questions of any kind
   - Disembodied numbers without consequence ("Fifteen megatons." means nothing alone — "Fifteen megatons and nothing hit the ground" is a paradox)
   - Fragments that require clip 02 to make sense ("Thirty-five miles per hour." — of what? why should I care?)
   - Throat-clearing preambles: "You have..." / "There are..." / "In [year]..."
   - Any hook where the viewer can scroll past without feeling they missed something impossible
   - Missing framing line — if clip 01 does not contain "You would not want to be", → FAIL

   **THE "WAIT, WHAT?" TEST.** Read your hook (Part A only) to someone with zero context. If they don't involuntarily say (or think) "wait, what?" or "how is that possible?" — the hook has failed. The hook must contain a contradiction, an absurdity, or a cruel irony that demands resolution. "Twenty-one people just died in a wave of molasses." — wait, what? "They added lifeboats to save lives. It killed eight hundred forty-four people." — how? "A thousand people went blind because they looked out their windows." — what?

   **VARIETY.** Each hook should find the single most "wait, what?" element of its specific topic. Do NOT default to leading with a number. Numbers are only compelling when paired with a consequence that makes them feel impossible. Across a series, hooks should feel like different fascinating stories, not the same template with different facts.
2. **THE IMMERSION** (clips 02-03): The framing line in clip 01 already told the viewer WHAT this video is about and WHEN. **FALSE SAFETY FIRST**: Clip 02 must open with at least one sentence of normalcy, comfort, or false hope — a moment the viewer can lose. "The ground has been still for weeks." "The morning is warm. You can hear birds." "The engineers said the dam would hold." THEN the danger arrives: "But beneath your feet —". This contrast is what creates dread. A script that starts at max danger has nowhere to go. After the false safety, introduce the specific physical reality that makes this disaster terrifying. Clip 03 tightens the trap with a specific detail that makes the danger feel inevitable. Include at least one **TINY DOMESTIC DETAIL** — a small, everyday, human-scale moment that grounds the disaster in reality: a coffee cup sliding, a crack in the wall, the dog barking at nothing. **FACT + FEELING**: Immersion MUST explain the specific mechanism that makes this disaster possible — but grounded in what YOU physically experience. Not "the tank holds two million gallons of molasses" (encyclopedia) but "the tank above your street holds two million gallons — and it has been groaning all winter" (story). The mechanism is real; the dread is personal.
3. **THE ATTEMPT** (clips 04-05): The viewer tries to survive and FAILS — each failure teaches one real fact. Agency verbs: "You run", "You warn", "You push toward the door." The attempt must be logical and historically grounded — something a real person would have tried. The failure must be specific. This is the emotional heart of the episode — the viewer HOPES, then loses that hope. **TENSION CHECKPOINT**: Include one sentence that explicitly signals escalation — a "the nightmare hadn't begun" moment that gives the viewer a beat to breathe before the catastrophe. "You think the worst is over. It is not." "The first wave was the small one." "That was only the foreshock." Sentences carry momentum ACROSS clip boundaries. **FACT + FEELING**: Show each attempt as a human decision with HOPE behind it — you try something because you believe it will work. The fact that kills the hope IS the teaching moment. Not "you cannot outrun it because the pyroclastic flow moves at 450 mph" (textbook) but "you run because you think you can make the tree line. The pyroclastic flow is faster than sound. You cannot hear what is about to kill you" (story). The viewer learns the physics THROUGH the death of their hope, not from a lecture.
4. **THE CATASTROPHE** (clips 06-07): The disaster hits. **HARD RHYTHM SHIFT**: switch from flowing sentences to brutal staccato punches — **short sentences averaging 5 words, maximum 8 words. Each sentence must CAUSE or REVEAL the next.** Not "X happens. Y happens. Z happens." (that's a word cloud) but "X hits. Y buckles because X hit. Z collapses — nothing held it up." The staccato rhythm is the impact — but even short sentences form a causal chain. The contrast with the flowing immersion is what creates the visceral impact. **FACT + FEELING**: At least one staccato sentence must land on YOU — not a statistic, but what happens to your body, your shelter, your hands. "Forty-three thousand civilians die" is a number the viewer scrolls past. "The concrete you trusted folds onto your chest" is a sentence they remember. Include one fact that recontextualizes scale — a number paired with a comparison that makes it visceral — but the catastrophe must HAPPEN TO YOU, not be observed from a helicopter.
5. **THE CONCLUSION** (clip 08, 8s): The devastating payoff. End with a definitive, closing statement that lands like a verdict — a final fact so stark it needs no follow-up. The callback is mandatory — the ending must echo or invert the hook for structural satisfaction. "The mountain did not need fire. It only needed gravity." The goal: leave the viewer STUNNED by the completeness of the destruction. The last sentence should feel like a door closing — not a question hanging, but an answer the viewer wishes they never heard. Closure IS the horror. **FACT + FEELING**: The callback must teach something the viewer didn't know at clip 01 — the final revelation reframes the entire episode. But it must land on YOU, not on a statistic. Not "fifteen thousand dead" (report) but "the volcano finished erupting before anyone perished — and you never saw it coming" (verdict delivered to the viewer). The conclusion is a sentence the viewer feels in their body, not just their brain.

### TikTok / Reels Retention Rules

This is TikTok/Reels/Shorts content. Retention is decided in 0.5 seconds. Apply these rules to EVERY word of narration:

- **No filler.** No throat-clearing. No scene-setting that could be cut without losing information. If a sentence doesn't teach a fact, create dread, or drive action, delete it.
- **No visual narration.** Never describe what the viewer can already see in the animation. "You look around" wastes 3 words. "The morning was beautiful" teaches nothing. The video handles the visuals — the narration handles the facts, the dread, and the action.
- **Front-load the punch.** The most arresting element of any sentence goes FIRST. Not "You have seven minutes before the sky turns black" — instead "Seven minutes. The sky turns black."
- **Interleave danger with exposition.** Never deliver more than one sentence of pure context without re-establishing threat. "Galveston, 1900. Thirty-seven thousand on a sandbar — and the water is rising at fourteen feet per hour."
- **Every second earns the next.** A viewer who made it to second 5 must feel that second 6 will be even more intense. No plateaus. No breathing room. Relentless escalation.
- **Word efficiency.** If a sentence can lose a word without losing information, lose the word. "You begin to run" → "You run." "The water starts to rise" → "The water rises." "It is at this point that" → delete entirely. Every word must earn its place at ~2.4 words per second.

**COMMON FILLER — BAN THESE PATTERNS:**
- "The morning was [adjective]" — scene-setting without danger
- "You find yourself in" — passive, wastes 4 words
- "It is [date]" — use "Date." as a fragment instead
- "You look around and see" — the video shows this, narration doesn't need to
- "Little do you know" — cliché, tells instead of shows
- "In the distance, you can see" — just state what's there

### NARRATIVE THREAD TEST — RUN THIS BEFORE RETURNING

After writing your full dialogue_script, read clips 01-08 aloud as ONE continuous paragraph (ignoring clip headers). Apply the DELETION TEST to every sentence after the hook:

**"If I delete this sentence, does the next sentence stop making sense?"**

If the answer is "no, the next sentence still works fine" — the deleted sentence is a factoid, not a story beat. It's decoration, not narrative. Rewrite it so it connects causally to the sentence before AND after it.

A script that passes the deletion test reads like: "A happened. Because of A, B happened. B meant C was now inevitable. You tried D, but C made D impossible. E struck because D failed." Every sentence is load-bearing.

A script that FAILS reads like: "A happened. B happened. C happened. You tried D. E happened." That is a word cloud — a list of facts about a topic arranged in chronological order. The viewer feels no forward pull because no sentence depends on the one before it.

**CAUSALITY WORDS to use**: "because", "so", "which means", "but", "— and that" — these are the joints of a story. If your script has zero causal connectors between clips 02-07, it is a list, not a narrative.

### SENSORY TEXTURE — WHAT DOES IT FEEL LIKE?

At least TWO sentences across the full script must describe what the disaster FEELS LIKE to the viewer's body — not what happens at scale, but what you physically experience. Heat on your skin. Dust filling your lungs. The floor shaking under your feet. Water rising past your ankles. The sound of something cracking above you. The smell of smoke.

These moments are what separate a story from a report. "The fire reaches eighteen hundred degrees" is a fact. "The heat blisters your hands before the flames reach you" is a sensation. Both teach the same thing — one the viewer processes with their brain, the other with their body.

**SENSORY ANTI-PATTERN — BANNED**: If every sentence in your script describes events from the sky looking down (bird's-eye statistics, citywide death tolls, total area destroyed) and zero sentences describe what the viewer FEELS at ground level, the script is a news report, not a story. Rewrite until the viewer is inside the disaster, not watching it on television.

### YOU IN THE CATASTROPHE — THE DISASTER HAPPENS TO YOU

In catastrophe clips (06-07) and the conclusion (08), "you" must be the SUBJECT of at least one sentence per clip — not just present as a pronoun somewhere, but the person the sentence is ABOUT. The disaster happens TO you, not around you while you watch from a distance.

**BANNED in catastrophe/conclusion**: Entire clips of third-person statistics with no "you" as subject. "Forty-three thousand civilians die. A million homes erased." — that's a news anchor reading casualties. Where are YOU in this? "The concrete folds onto your chest. Forty-three thousand share your fate." — now it's a story. The statistic hits harder BECAUSE the viewer felt it personally first.

### SUSPENSE ARCHITECTURE — HOW NATGEO BUILDS DREAD

Real National Geographic documentaries create suspense through CONTRAST — the calm before the storm is what makes the storm devastating. A script that starts at maximum danger and stays there has nowhere to go. These four techniques are mandatory:

**1. FALSE SAFETY.** The Immersion beat (clips 02-03) must contain at least one sentence that establishes normalcy, comfort, or false hope BEFORE the danger arrives. The viewer needs something to LOSE. NatGeo's Tsunami doc: "After four years of war, Hawaiians can relax. At last, their island paradise is safe from attack. But more than 2,000 miles away, a new threat emerges from the sea." The safety makes the wave devastating. Without it, the wave is just a fact. Not "The earthquake strikes at 5:46 AM" (no contrast) but "The morning is still. You can hear birds. Then the floor tilts." (contrast).

**2. TINY DOMESTIC DETAIL.** At least one sentence across the script must describe a small, human-scale, everyday detail — not the disaster at scale, but the quiet moment before or during it. A survivor from the 1946 Hawaii tsunami: "I was brushing my teeth when I heard this strange sound of gravel." THAT is what makes the viewer think "that could be me." Not grand vistas of destruction but the coffee cup sliding off the table, the dog barking at nothing, the crack in the wall that wasn't there this morning. One tiny detail grounds the entire script in reality.

**3. TENSION CHECKPOINT.** In the Attempt beat (clips 04-05), include one sentence that explicitly signals escalation — a "the nightmare hadn't begun yet" moment. This gives the viewer a beat to breathe before the catastrophe hits harder. "You think the worst is over. It is not." "The first wave was the small one." "That was only the foreshock." Kurzgesagt uses this devastatingly: "Had it stopped here, there might still be dinosaurs today. But the nightmare hadn't even begun yet." The pause before the escalation IS the suspense.

**4. COMPARISON ANCHOR.** When stating a number (temperature, speed, death toll, pressure), pair it with a comparison the viewer already understands. Numbers alone are abstract — comparisons make them visceral. Not "eighteen hundred degrees" alone but "eighteen hundred degrees — hotter than the inside of a jet engine." Not "thirty-five miles per hour" alone but "thirty-five miles per hour — faster than you can sprint." NatGeo's Asteroids doc: "Like taking all the world's nuclear weapons, putting them all in one pile and setting them all off at once... actually, it'd be a little bit more energy than that." The casual "actually" is what makes the number unforgettable. At least one number in your script MUST have a comparison anchor.

## DIALOGUE SCRIPT REQUIREMENTS (ElevenLabs Narration — continuous prose, chunked in post)

Write the full narration as a **continuous prose narrative** with soft clip markers. This script is generated as ONE continuous audio file by ElevenLabs TTS, then split into per-clip chunks using word-level timestamps in post-production. It is NOT embedded in Veo prompts.

The narrator is a calm, authoritative British naturalist — a National Geographic documentary voice narrating apocalyptic history. Warm curiosity. Measured gravitas. Scientific precision. Genuine awe at the scale of forces involved. The horror comes from the steady, composed delivery of devastating facts, not from snark.

### CRITICAL: Continuous Narration Architecture

The narration is ONE continuous audio track that flows across all clip boundaries. Sentences MAY and SHOULD span clip boundaries — this is how real documentaries work. The clip markers below are **soft visual sync suggestions**, not hard audio boundaries. When the final video is stitched together, the full narration is overlaid as a single unbroken track — the viewer hears one continuous narration over 8 visual cuts.

Write as if telling ONE continuous story. Do NOT write 8 isolated micro-scripts. Each clip's narration should flow naturally from the previous one — a sentence can start in clip 03 and finish in clip 04.

Format:

```
## Clip 01 (0:00-0:04) — The Hook
NARRATOR: The mountain has already stopped erupting. That is not what kills you. You would not want to be at Mount Unzen in 1792.

## Clip 02 (0:04-0:12) — Immersion
NARRATOR: The ground has been still for weeks. You can smell the sea from your door. But this morning a crack appears in your garden wall —

## Clip 03 (0:12-0:16) — Immersion
NARRATOR: — and the entire eastern face of the mountain is sliding toward you.

## Clip 04 (0:16-0:24) — Attempt
NARRATOR: You run uphill because you think the coast is the danger. The dome moves at sixty miles per hour — faster than a galloping horse —

## Clip 05 (0:24-0:28) — Attempt
NARRATOR: — so you turn toward the sea. The water has pulled away. That is not a good sign.

## Clip 06 (0:28-0:32) — Catastrophe
NARRATOR: The landslide hits the bay. The wave comes back for you.

## Clip 07 (0:32-0:40) — Catastrophe
NARRATOR: Thirty metres tall. You hear it before you see it. Five thousand on this shore. Five thousand on the other.

## Clip 08 (0:40-0:48) — Conclusion
NARRATOR: Fifteen thousand dead. The volcano had finished erupting a month before anyone perished. The mountain did not need fire. It only needed gravity.
```

Note how sentences cross clip boundaries with dash continuations. This is intentional and correct — the audio will be one continuous stream, and the visual cut happens mid-sentence. This creates a feeling of relentless forward momentum.

### Narration Rules:

- **CONTINUOUS PROSE.** Write one unbroken narrative, not 8 isolated factoids. The narration should read as a single flowing piece of documentary writing. Clip markers are soft sync points, not hard walls.
- **Sentences span clip boundaries.** A sentence can start in one clip and finish in the next. Use em dashes (—) at clip boundaries to indicate mid-sentence continuity. This is standard documentary technique — the visual cut happens while the narrator is mid-thought, pulling the viewer forward.
- **Nature documentary tone.** Calm, measured, authoritative. British naturalist guiding you through the extraordinary. Genuine reverence for the forces involved. Scientific precision delivered with warm curiosity and quiet awe. NOT dark comedy, NOT gallows humor, NOT snark.
- **Story first, facts second.** Every beat must include at least one specific, verifiable fact — but the fact must be FELT, not just stated. Not "the earth's crust fractures along a 300-mile fault line" (textbook) but "the ground splits beneath your feet along a fracture three hundred miles long" (experience). The fact is identical; the delivery is personal. If a sentence could appear in a Wikipedia article unchanged, it belongs in a Wikipedia article, not in this script.
- **Present tense ONLY.** "You wake up" not "You've arrived." Immediacy is everything.
- **Sentence rhythm varies by beat.** Immersion and Attempt use longer, flowing sentences that build the world. Catastrophe clips (06-07) use brutal staccato punches — **sentences averaging 5 words, maximum 8 words, each causing or revealing the next**. The rhythm shift IS the pacing. If your Catastrophe clips don't have sentences averaging 4-6 words while your Immersion clips average 12-20, you have failed. But even staccato sentences must form a CAUSAL CHAIN — "The surge arrives. It boils the harbour. The steam blinds you." — not disconnected factoids.
- **Total: 95-105 words** at ~2.4 words/second (measured ElevenLabs pacing) across 8 clips. This produces ~40-44 seconds of narration to fit within the ~45-second effective video window (48s raw minus crossfade transitions and narration delay). COUNT YOUR WORDS BEFORE RETURNING THE JSON. If your total exceeds 105, cut words until it doesn't. If under 95, the script is too thin — add sensory detail.
- **Voice on frame 1.** Clip 01 (the hook) starts with a devastating statement — NO silent openings. NEVER a question.
- **Clip 01 MUST include the framing line** "You would not want to be in the [Event], [Year]" immediately after the hook. This is how the viewer knows what the video is about. The framing line is narrated only — clip 02 now goes straight into danger.
- **Second person throughout**: "you", "your" — the viewer IS the skeleton.
- **Agency verbs**: "You run", "You grab", "You shout" — not passive observation.
- **Tension ratchet**: Every act must raise the stakes from the previous one. No plateaus. No breathing room.
- **Sensory continuity**: If clip 02 establishes smoke, clip 03 still has smoke. The environment accumulates — it does not reset between clips.
- **Narrative thread**: Every sentence connects to the one before it through causation, consequence, or escalation. Not "Fact A. Fact B. Fact C." but "Fact A, which causes B, which means C is now inevitable." The narration has causality, not just sequence. Test: for each sentence after the hook, ask "why does this follow from the previous sentence?" If the answer is "it doesn't, it's just another fact about this topic," rewrite until the chain is unbroken.
- **Sensory specificity**: What does it FEEL like to be there? Not just numbers but physical sensation — heat on skin, dust in lungs, the floor shaking, the sound of something cracking above you. "Eighteen hundred degrees" is a number. "The heat blisters your hands before the flames reach you" is a story. Both teach — one the viewer processes with their brain, the other with their body.
- **One moment of genuine scientific awe per episode**: A fact so extraordinary it creates wonder — delivered through the viewer's experience, not as a footnote.
- **Callback ending**: The twist/payoff should echo or invert the hook for structural satisfaction.
- **Definitive ending**: End with a closing statement that lands like a verdict on YOU — not a statistic about a population, but a sentence the viewer feels in their chest. Closure IS the punch.
- **BAN: Pure-statistic catastrophe sentences.** If a catastrophe or conclusion sentence contains ONLY a number and a noun ("Forty-three thousand civilians die. A million homes erased.") with no "you" and no sensory grounding, it is a news report, not a story. Rewrite: either put "you" inside it ("The concrete you trusted folds onto your chest. Forty-three thousand share your fate.") or attach a sensory consequence ("Forty-three thousand die. The dust does not settle for three days."). Numbers hit harder after the viewer has felt the disaster personally.
- **Historical accuracy**: Real dates, real numbers, real consequences. Every fact checkable.
- The narration in the dialogue script is produced as a single continuous ElevenLabs audio file — it does NOT appear in Veo video prompts.

```
## Clip 04 (0:16-0:24) — Attempt
NARRATOR: You run uphill because you think the coast is the danger. The dome moves at sixty miles per hour — faster than a galloping horse —

## Clip 05 (0:24-0:28) — Attempt
NARRATOR: — so you turn toward the sea. The water has pulled away. That is not a good sign.

## Clip 06 (0:28-0:32) — Catastrophe
NARRATOR: The landslide hits the bay. The wave comes back for you.

## Clip 07 (0:32-0:40) — Catastrophe
NARRATOR: Thirty metres tall. You hear it before you see it. Five thousand on this shore. Five thousand on the other.

## Clip 08 (0:40-0:48) — Conclusion
```

WHY THIS WORKS: **False safety**: "The ground has been still for weeks. ### Example B: The Galveston Hurricane — 1900 (The experts said it would miss — but you're trapped)

```

NARRATOR: — but the Weather Bureau refuses to warn you. The surf swallows the railway tracks before anyone thinks to leave.

## Clip 08 (0:40-0:48) — Conclusion
```

WHY THIS WORKS: **False safety**: "Children are playing on the beach this morning" — an image of normalcy about to be destroyed. **Tiny detail**: children on the beach is the domestic moment the viewer can picture. **Tension checkpoint**: "The storm has not even made landfall yet" — everything terrible so far is the WARM-UP. **Comparison anchor**: built into the hook — "eight feet above the sea" vs a fifteen-foot surge. **Callback**: "you were eight feet above the sea."
