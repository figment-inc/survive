## THE FOUR RULES

### 1. THE HOOK MUST BE IMMEDIATELY CLEAR

State the most interesting thing about this event plainly and immediately. The viewer should understand the premise in the first sentence. No riddles, no cryptic fragments, no withholding.

BAD: "Twenty engines. Seven hundred men. Not one can move." — The viewer has no idea what's happening. They're confused, not intrigued.

GOOD: "Every fire engine in Boston needs horses to move. Then every horse in the city gets sick." — The viewer instantly understands the premise and wants to know what happens next.

The hook is NOT a puzzle for the viewer to solve. The hook IS the interesting thing, stated plainly. If a stranger scrolling TikTok reads your first sentence, they should immediately think "oh no, that's a problem" — not "wait, what?"

### 2. EACH SENTENCE MUST MAKE THE VIEWER SMARTER

Every sentence in the explanation should build understanding. When the viewer finishes, they should be able to explain to a friend WHY this disaster happened — not just THAT it happened.

BAD: "Tarred roofs catch like matchheads. Embers land on your shoulders." — Atmospheric, but the viewer learns nothing about why the fire was unstoppable.

GOOD: "Tarred rooftops are already burning. Fire jumps block to block faster than you can pull a four-tonne machine." — The viewer understands the mechanism: the fire is faster than the response.

Spend words on UNDERSTANDING, not atmosphere. A viewer who can explain the disaster to a friend has been served well. A viewer who remembers vivid imagery but can't explain what happened has been failed.

### 3. THE ENDING MUST GIVE REAL CLOSURE

Give the viewer the full picture: the scale of what happened, why it was so terrible, and one final line that makes them genuinely grateful they weren't there. Not a clever callback. Not wordplay. Real closure.

BAD: "The best fire department in America, beaten by a flu." — Clever, but the viewer doesn't feel the scale or understand why it matters.

GOOD: "Seven hundred buildings burned in twelve hours. The best fire department in America, and it couldn't save a single one — because every horse was sick." — The viewer gets the scale (700 buildings, 12 hours), the irony (best department), and the cause (sick horses) all in one beat.

### 4. EVERY STORY NEEDS A HUMAN MISTAKE

The best scripts make the viewer watch someone do something reasonable — and then watch that decision kill them. "The captain turns on the lights" is devastating BECAUSE it was sensible. "A 9.1 earthquake struck" is a fact — nobody chose it, nobody could have prevented it.

Find the human decision (or institutional failure) that made this disaster worse than it had to be. Thread it through the story. The viewer should finish thinking "if only they hadn't..."

BAD: "A magnitude 9.1 earthquake ruptured the seafloor, displacing a wall of water." — Facts. No one made a choice. The viewer is informed but not gutted.

GOOD: "The Pacific has tsunami sirens. The Indian Ocean has none — because no one believed a tsunami could happen here." — An institution made a bet. The bet was wrong. Thousands died.

If the event is purely geological or natural, find the institutional bet, the ignored warning, the cost-saving shortcut, or the evacuation that never happened. There is ALWAYS a human fingerprint on the death toll.

## STRUCTURE — 3 BEATS

1. **HOOK** (clip 01) — State the premise clearly and immediately. The viewer should understand what this episode is about from the first sentence. Then the framing line.

2. **THE STORY** (middle clips) — Tell the story so the viewer genuinely understands what happened and why. Setup explains how things work (the context the viewer needs). Escalation shows what goes wrong and why. Catastrophe is short, visceral, physical. "You" is suffering.

3. **THE ENDING** (final clip) — The full picture. Scale + cause + closure. The viewer finishes feeling "thank god I wasn't there" — not because of a clever line, but because they genuinely understand how terrible it was.

## EXAMPLE A — The Great Boston Fire, 1872
(FATAL DECISION: The city built its entire fire response around horses — no backup plan.)

## Clip 04 — Catastrophe

## Clip 05 — Ending
NARRATOR: Seven hundred buildings burned in twelve hours — because every horse was sick.

## Clip 04 — Catastrophe

## Clip 05 — Ending
