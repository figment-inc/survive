## THE SPOKEN-ALOUD TEST — READ THIS FIRST

Read every sentence out loud in your head. If it sounds unfinished, fragmented, or like a telegram, it is broken. This is narration for a documentary — not captions, not bullet points, not text messages. Every sentence must be grammatically complete and flow naturally into the next when spoken.

BAD (fragments): "You claw for the hatch. Netting blocks it. Anti-boarding mesh. You pull. The knots hold."

GOOD (flowing prose): "You claw for the hatch, but rope netting blocks it — anti-boarding mesh, lashed tight across the deck. You pull. The knots hold."

The BAD version reads like a grocery list. The GOOD version reads like someone telling you a story. Exception: clip 04 (Catastrophe) CAN use short punchy sentences for rhythmic impact — but even those must be complete sentences, not fragments.

## WORD COUNT — CUT FIRST

The final script MUST be 75-90 words (count only words after "NARRATOR:"). HARD MAXIMUM: 90 words. At ~2.6 wps, this fills ~35 seconds.

If the draft exceeds 90 words, your FIRST action is to cut — before fixing any other issues. Remove any sentence that doesn't advance the causal chain. Merge two-sentence explanations into one. Cut whole clips if needed — 4 clips at 80 words beats 5 clips at 130. Every word over 90 is screen time you don't have.

PROTECTED BEATS: When cutting for word count, NEVER cut the mechanism — the sentence(s) that explain WHY the situation changed from setup to catastrophe. This is the educational core of the script. If the mechanism is 25 words and you need to cut 15, cut atmosphere, cut sensory detail, shorten the hook, compress the catastrophe — but the mechanism stays.

The mechanism is the answer to: "What went wrong and why?" If you can't point to a sentence in your rewrite that answers this question, you've cut too deep.

## THE THREE PRIORITIES

### 1. HOOK CLARITY (fix first)

The hook must state the most interesting thing about this event plainly and immediately. The viewer must understand the premise from the first sentence. The framing line ("You would not want to be in...") MUST come LAST in Clip 01 — never first. If the draft opens with the framing line, move it to the end of the clip and put the conflict premise first.

BAD (framing line first): "You would not want to be a Roman legionary in the Teutoburg Forest, 9 AD. Fifteen thousand soldiers are marching..." — 12 words of branding before the viewer hears anything interesting.
GOOD (premise first): "A safety valve is removed for maintenance. Nobody tells the night shift. You would not want to be on Piper Alpha, 1988." — Conflict hooks immediately, framing line anchors it.

### 2. UNDERSTANDING (fix second)

Every sentence must build the viewer's understanding of WHY this disaster happened. If the Critic flagged Understanding below 7, this is your top priority after Hook Clarity.

BAD: "Tarred roofs catch like matchheads. Embers land on your shoulders." — Atmospheric, but the viewer learns nothing about why the fire was unstoppable.
GOOD: "Tarred rooftops are already burning. Fire jumps block to block faster than you can pull a four-tonne machine." — The viewer understands the mechanism.

Cut sensory filler to make room for explanatory sentences. Atmosphere is nice. Understanding is mandatory.

### 3. CLOSURE (fix third)

The ending must be a REVELATION that recontextualizes the story — not a summary that signals wrap-up. The viewer's brain should still be processing when the video cuts.

NEVER open the final clip with a death toll number. A number tells the viewer "we're summarizing now" and their thumb starts the swipe. Lead with the cause or the irony — land the scale INSIDE the revelation.

BAD (summary): "492 died in fifteen minutes — every door locked to save a few dollars on tabs." — Death toll first signals the video is ending.
BAD: "The city never recovered." — Vague.
GOOD (revelation): "Every door was locked to save a few dollars on bar tabs. 492 people never made it out." — Irony first, scale lands as the gut punch.
GOOD: "Two hundred thousand soldiers couldn't break those walls. They walked through an unlocked door." — Still processing when the video cuts.

## TENSION TEST — APPLY AFTER EVERY REWRITE

Read each clip ending aloud. At the end of each clip, the viewer should be thinking "oh no, what happens next?" If a clip ends and the viewer thinks "okay, and?" — that clip needs a hook-forward: a detail that creates anticipation for the next beat.

Examples of hook-forwards:
- "The ship has no escort." — viewer immediately wonders what will happen to it
- "Fire jumps block to block faster than you can pull a four-tonne machine." — viewer knows this is about to get worse
- "On the last night, someone leaves it unlatched." — viewer dreads what's coming through that door

If no human decision or institutional failure appears anywhere in the script, you MUST inject one. Find the cost-saving shortcut, the ignored warning, the policy gap, or the reasonable choice that turned fatal. Thread it through the story so the viewer finishes thinking "if only they hadn't..."
