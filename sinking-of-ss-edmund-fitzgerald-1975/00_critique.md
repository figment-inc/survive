## LINE-LEVEL CRITIQUE

### Narrator Restraint — 5/10

**Failing line (Clip 03):** "You radio the ship behind you — you say you're holding your own."

**Problem:** This is a famous last transmission, and framing it right before the catastrophe clip functions as dramatic irony. The narrator is effectively saying "here's the moment of false confidence before the disaster." The viewer knows exactly what's coming. It's subtle foreshadowing, but it's foreshadowing — the placement and phrasing signal doom.

**Fix:** Make this a factual status update that feels routine rather than ominous: "You radio the Arthur Anderson behind you — your pumps are running, your bow is still low." This keeps the communication beat without the ironic weight of "holding your own" right before death.

### Educational Completeness — 7/10

**Failing line (Clip 01):** "Your captain radios that a fence rail is down and two vents are gone — your hull is open to the lake."

**Problem:** The viewer hears "fence rail" and "vents" but doesn't understand why these matter structurally. A fence rail sounds trivial. The leap to "your hull is open" needs one beat of context — these vents and covers are what keep Lake Superior out of your cargo holds. Without that, the causal chain's first link is shaky.

**Fix:** "Your captain radios that vent covers are damaged — lake water is pouring into your cargo holds." This eliminates the unexplained "fence rail" and makes the mechanism instantly clear.
