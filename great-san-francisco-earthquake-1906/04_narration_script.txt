What happens if the ground beneath your city just... stops being solid? You wake in darkness. The bed is moving. Not rocking — bucking. The walls scream. San Francisco, April eighteenth, nineteen oh six. Five twelve AM. Forty-seven seconds that erase everything. The street ripples like water. Cobblestones bounce. The earth has forgotten how to be earth. The shaking stops. The silence is worse. South of Market is gone. Wooden buildings, pancaked. And from three directions, smoke. The gas mains are broken. The fires have already started. You find a firefighter. He opens a hydrant. Nothing. Not a drop. Three hundred water mains, shattered. The fire moves faster than you can run. Entire blocks ignite at once. The heat is so intense, buildings catch fire from fifty feet away. No water. No plan. Just wind and flame. The Army tries dynamite. They blow up buildings to make firebreaks. The embers sail right over. Worse — the blasts spread the fire further. They are saving the city by destroying it. Nob Hill is burning. Mansions that took decades to build vanish in minutes. Fire does not care about money. Three days. The fire burns for three days. Twenty-eight thousand buildings. Over three thousand souls. Two hundred and twenty-seven thousand people with nothing. The city that thought it was invincible, leveled to ash. And there you sit. In the centre of absence. They will rebuild, of course. They always do. But in the rubble beneath Market Street, they will find a pocket watch, stopped at five twelve. Some moments, it turns out, last forever.
