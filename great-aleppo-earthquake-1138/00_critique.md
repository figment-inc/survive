Fails on Educational Completeness (5), Narrative Thread (5), and Escalation Curve (5) — three axes below the threshold. Average also falls short of 7.0.

## LINE-LEVEL CRITIQUE

### Narrative Thread — 5/10

**Failing line (Clip 01):** "Every building in Aleppo is heavy stone stacked on mud mortar."
**Failing line (Clip 02):** "You live in a house built to stop swords, not tremors — heavy stone on mud mortar that shatters under any sideways force"
**Problem:** These two clips deliver the same fact in nearly the same words. The script burns half its runtime saying "stone on mud mortar" twice. Clip 02 should be ADVANCING the story — adding a new piece of information that makes the situation worse — not rephrasing the hook.
**Fix:** Keep the mechanism in Clip 01. Use Clip 02 to introduce the density/urban layout problem that turns one collapse into a chain reaction: "Your house shares walls with every neighbor on the block. Thirty thousand homes, shoulder to shoulder, with no open ground between them."

### Escalation Curve — 5/10

**Failing line (Clip 02):** "heavy stone on mud mortar that shatters under any sideways force, walls packed so tight the whole block moves as one"
**Problem:** Because Clip 02 restates Clip 01, the emotional register is flat for the first 15 seconds. The escalation only begins at Clip 03, giving you a two-clip ramp instead of a three-clip ramp. The viewer is bored before the earthquake even hits.
**Fix:** Clip 02 should introduce a second, distinct danger factor (density, narrow streets, no open ground) so the viewer feels the trap tightening BEFORE the quake arrives. Then Clip 03's earthquake feels like the trigger on a loaded gun, not the gun and the trigger at once.

### Educational Completeness — 5/10

**Failing lines (Clips 03–05):** "At dawn, the ground lurches" → "The citadel collapses. Your stone roof pancakes downward." → "You are one of 230,000 dead"
**Problem:** The causal chain jumps from "earthquake happens" straight to "230,000 dead." The script never explains the intermediate mechanism: WHY did so many die? Was it the density (buildings sharing walls, so one collapse triggers the next)? Was it the narrow streets (no escape routes, rubble fills the alleys)? Was it the lack of any rescue capacity? The viewer knows stone-on-mud is bad, but can't explain why THIS earthquake killed more people than almost any other in medieval history. They'd tell a friend "the buildings were bad" — which is true but incomplete.
**Fix:** Insert the chain reaction mechanism into Clip 03 or 04: "One wall falls into the next — shared walls drag whole blocks down in sequence. The streets are too narrow to run; rubble fills them before you reach open ground." Now the viewer understands density + shared walls + narrow streets = catastrophic chain collapse.

### Understanding — 6/10

**Failing line (Clip 02):** "walls packed so tight the whole block moves as one"
**Problem:** This hints at the chain-collapse mechanism but doesn't explain it. "Moves as one" could mean they sway together, or collapse together, or shift on their foundations. The viewer doesn't learn the specific mechanism — shared load-bearing walls mean one failure propagates instantly through the block. Also, no human decision is visible beyond "they didn't build for earthquakes." Who chose this building style, and why? The script says "built to stop swords" — that's the seed of a human decision (military priority over seismic safety), but it's never developed.
**Fix:** Make the military-architecture tradeoff explicit: "Aleppo's rulers built for sieges — thick walls, narrow streets, no open squares an army could use. Every choice that kept invaders out now traps you inside."

### Word Count — 6/10

**Word count:** 94 words — 4 over the sweet spot ceiling.
**Problem:** The redundancy between Clips 01 and 02 is the easiest source of cuts. Eliminating the repeated "heavy stone on mud mortar" from Clip 02 saves ~8 words, which can be reinvested in the missing mechanism.
**Fix:** Cut the restated material from Clip 02 and use those words for the chain-collapse explanation.
