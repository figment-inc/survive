## LINE-LEVEL CRITIQUE

### Hook Impact — 6/10

**Failing line (Clip 01):** "Forty tonnes of poison gas. Every safety system turned off."
**Problem:** These two facts are sequential and complementary — poison + no safety = disaster. That's cause and effect, not paradox. The viewer thinks "that's terrible" not "wait, what?" A paradox would juxtapose two truths that seem impossible together — e.g., the plant was designed to be safe vs. the systems were deliberately disabled.
**Fix:** "The plant had six independent safety systems. On December 3rd, every single one was off." — Now the viewer's brain snags: how can a plant with six safety systems have zero working? That's a paradox.

---

### Context Clarity — 5/10

**Failing line (Clip 03):** "The flare tower is off. The scrubber is off."
**Problem:** A viewer who has never heard of Bhopal does not know what a flare tower or a scrubber does. These are technical terms dropped without function. The viewer hears two nouns and cannot feel why they matter. You must explain the mechanism before the consequence.
**Fix:** "The flare tower that burns off leaking gas — off. The scrubber that neutralises it — off." — Now each noun carries its function. The viewer understands what was lost.

**Failing line (Clip 03):** "Water leaks into a tank — and the chemical boils."
**Problem:** Why does water make it boil? The viewer has no mental model for an exothermic reaction with MIC. This is a gap that requires the audience to already know the chemistry.
**Fix:** "Water leaks into the tank. Methyl isocyanate reacts with water violently — the temperature rockets, and the chemical becomes gas." — Mechanism before consequence.

**Failing line (Clip 02):** "a chemical called methyl isocyanate is stored in massive tanks — it kills on contact with your lungs."
**Problem:** Minor, but "stored in massive tanks" doesn't explain why storage matters. The volume is the danger, but the script doesn't connect quantity to lethality.
**Fix:** "Forty tonnes of methyl isocyanate sit in underground tanks. One breath of it destroys your lungs." — Quantity + consequence linked.

### You-As-Subject — 5/10

**Failing line (Clip 02):** "A Union Carbide pesticide plant sits at the edge of your neighbourhood. Inside, a chemical called methyl isocyanate is stored in massive tanks"
**Problem:** "You" appears only as a possessive ("your neighbourhood"). The plant sits, the chemical is stored — these are third-person descriptions of infrastructure. The viewer is a spectator, not a participant.
**Fix:** "You live three hundred metres from a pesticide plant. You breathe the same air as forty tonnes of methyl isocyanate." — "You" is the subject, doing, existing in proximity.

**Failing line (Clip 03):** "To cut costs, the refrigeration keeping it stable is shut down. The flare tower is off. The scrubber is off. Water leaks into a tank — and the chemical boils."
**Problem:** Zero "you" in the entire clip. This is a corporate engineering report. The disaster is happening to equipment, not to a person.
**Fix:** "You don't know the refrigeration keeping it stable has been shut off. You don't know the flare tower and scrubber are both off. Water leaks into the tank. The chemical boils." — "You" is the subject, and the ignorance IS the horror.

**Failing line (Clip 05):** "Three thousand dead by morning. The safety systems that could have contained it were all there. Every one had been switched off to save money."
**Problem:** "You" vanishes entirely in the payoff. The disaster happened to a statistic ("three thousand dead") and to equipment ("safety systems"). The viewer exits the body they were placed in.
**Fix:** "You survive — three thousand of your neighbours don't. Every safety system that could have saved them was there. Every one had been switched off to save money." — "You" persists, and the viewer carries survivor weight.

### Twist Payoff Power — 6/10

**Failing line (Clip 05):** "The safety systems that could have contained it were all there. Every one had been switched off to save money."
**Problem:** The hook already said "Every safety system turned off." The payoff adds "to save money" — which is new — but the core revelation is a repetition, not a reframe. A strong payoff should make the viewer re-hear the hook with *different* meaning. Here they re-hear it with the *same* meaning plus a motive. That's an addendum, not a twist.
**Fix:** Restructure the hook to emphasize the plant's design safety (paradox of engineering perfection), then the payoff reveals that perfection was manually undone for cost savings. Hook: "Six safety systems. Not one failed." Payoff: "Not one failed. Every one was switched off." — Now "not one failed" flips from reassurance to indictment.
