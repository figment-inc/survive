## LINE-LEVEL CRITIQUE

### Sensory Immersion — 5/10

**Failing line (Clip 02):** "For two years, you have shaped oak ribs for a vessel meant to terrify the Baltic."
**Problem:** "Shaped oak ribs" is the closest we get to physical sensation in the entire first half, and it's abstract — there's no smell of fresh-cut wood, no ache in the arms, no splinter, no sweat. The viewer's body is idle.
**Fix:** "For two years, you have swung an adze into green oak until your shoulders burn. The ribs of the hull curve like a cathedral — and smell like one, fresh-sawn and soaked in pitch."

**Failing line (Clip 07):** "A gust catches the sails. The Vasa heels to port. Water pours through the open gun ports —"
**Problem:** This is the catastrophic moment and the viewer is watching from a drone. Where's the deck lurching under your feet? The lurch that throws you into the gunwale? The cold Baltic water hitting your ankles? The script gives us visual information, not body sensation.
**Fix:** "A gust hits. The deck tilts under your feet and does not stop. You grab a stay line — your palms burn on wet rope. Cold water rushes across your boots, your knees, your waist."

**Failing line (Clip 08):** "You hear timber groan, then silence."
**Problem:** "Hear timber groan" is the one sensory beat in the catastrophe, and it's followed by "silence," which is the absence of sensation. The flooding of an entire gun deck should be deafening, freezing, suffocating — the body should be overwhelmed, not listening.
**Fix:** "The cold hits your chest like a wall. You hear timber crack, rigging snap, then the water closes over your head and there is nothing but dark and pressure."

### You-As-Subject — 5/10

**Failing line (Clip 03):** "Sixty-four bronze cannons across two gun decks. The king wants more guns, higher castles, more gilt. Every order adds weight above the waterline."
**Problem:** No "you" in the entire clip. This is a Wikipedia entry about the ship's specifications. The viewer is reading a fact sheet, not living a life.
**Fix:** "You mount sixty-four bronze cannons across two gun decks. The king wants more guns, higher castles, more gilt. Every order you carry out adds weight above the waterline."

**Failing line (Clip 05):** "The admiral watches. He says nothing. The king is abroad, waging war, expecting his flagship. No one writes the letter that says she is not safe."
**Problem:** The admiral watches. The king wages war. "No one" writes the letter. "You" has entirely disappeared at the moment of maximum moral weight — the silence that kills everyone. This is where "you" should be complicit.
**Fix:** "The admiral watches. He says nothing. The king is abroad, expecting his flagship. You could write the letter that says she is not safe. You do not write it."

**Failing line (Clip 09):** "She sinks in thirty-two metres of water, masts still above the surface, flags still flying. Around you, people who cannot swim cling to wreckage in full view of the city."
**Problem:** "She sinks" — the ship is the subject. "People cling" — other people are the subject. "You" is reduced to a spatial preposition ("around you"). The viewer is a camera, not a drowning person.
**Fix:** "You surface. The masts still stand above the water, flags still flying. You cling to wreckage. Around you, children scream for parents who went under. The whole city watches from the waterfront."

**Failing line (Clip 10):** "Thirty souls are lost. At the inquiry, every man blames the man above him. The shipwright blames the admiral. The admiral blames the king. The king blames God."
**Problem:** "You" disappears entirely in the aftermath. "The shipwright" is doing the blaming — but that shipwright is supposed to be YOU.
**Fix:** "Thirty souls are lost. At the inquiry, you blame the admiral. The admiral blames the king. The king blames God. No one blames the silence."

**Failing line (Clip 11):** "She carried more firepower than any warship on earth. She was top-heavy by design, unsafe by committee, and launched by silence."
**Problem:** "She" is the subject of every clause. This is an obituary for a ship, not for the people who built and sailed her. "You" has been absent for three consecutive clips.
**Fix:** "You built the most powerful warship on earth. You made her top-heavy by design, unsafe by committee, and launched her with your silence."

### Rhythm Shift — 6/10

**Failing line (Clip 08):** "The weight holds her down. The Baltic floods the lower deck in seconds. You hear timber groan, then silence."
**Problem:** These sentences run 6, 9, and 7 words — which is decent staccato, but the preceding clip (07) has similar sentence lengths ("A gust catches the sails" = 5 words, "The Vasa heels to port" = 6 words). There's no rhythmic *contrast* between the turn and the catastrophe. And Clip 09 immediately returns to long flowing sentences (17 words, 16 words), which deflates the staccato before it lands.
**Fix:** Make Clip 07 the last flowing sentence — let the em-dash be the breaking point. Then Clip 08 goes hard staccato:
"She does not come back. Water fills the gun deck. The weight drags her down. Cold. Dark. Your hands find nothing to hold."
And keep Clip 09 short: "You surface. Masts above the water. Flags still flying. Children screaming. The whole city stares."

**Failing line (Clip 09):** "She sinks in thirty-two metres of water, masts still above the surface, flags still flying. Around you, people who cannot swim cling to wreckage in full view of the city."
**Problem:** Two long, flowing sentences in what should be the peak of catastrophe. The rhythm has returned to the pacing of Clips 02–04. The viewer's pulse should still be racing; instead the prose is calm, elegiac, observational.
**Fix:** See rewrite above — fragment everything. No sentence over 6 words in this clip.

### Narrator Restraint — 7/10

This scores at the threshold but two lines deserve flagging for a rewrite pass:

**Borderline line (Clip 04):** "You know something is wrong."
**Problem:** This is the narrator telling the viewer what to feel before showing the evidence. The stability test that follows is powerful enough on its own. "You know something is wrong" is a stage direction, not narration.
**Fix:** Cut it. Open with: "Thirty men run back and forth across the deck — a stability test." The wrongness is self-evident.

**Borderline line (Clip 11):** "She was top-heavy by design, unsafe by committee, and launched by silence."
**Problem:** "Launched by silence" is the narrator editorializing with a literary flourish. It's a thesis statement, not a fact. The script already showed the silence in Clip 05 — restating it as commentary weakens it.
**Fix:** If Clip 05 is rewritten with "You do not write it," the silence lands through action. Clip 11 can then stay factual: "You built the most powerful warship on earth. She drew less than five metres of freeboard. Every man who knew stayed quiet."
