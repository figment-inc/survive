## LINE-LEVEL CRITIQUE

### Educational Completeness — 6/10

**Failing line (Clip 03):** "The sea is gone — debris from the eruption shoved it back."
**Problem:** This is the script's weakest causal link. "Debris shoved it back" doesn't give the viewer a mechanism they can repeat. Pyroclastic flows entering the bay? Volcanic landslide displacing water? The viewer hears "debris" and doesn't know what kind, how it reached the sea, or why the sea then "returns" as a tsunami. That's a full link missing from the chain.
**Fix:** "Pyroclastic flows slam into the bay. The sea pulls back from the shore — then returns as a wall of water." Now the viewer sees: flows hit water → displacement → tsunami. Explainable in one sentence.

### Escalation Curve — 6/10 (borderline 7)

**Failing line (Clip 03):** "You reach the shore. The sea is gone — debris from the eruption shoved it back. Then it returns."
**Problem:** Three catastrophic beats in four seconds. The tsunami should be the TURN — the moment the viewer thinks "they escaped the mud" and then realizes the coast is worse. But it's compressed into a dependent clause and a two-word sentence. There's no beat of false hope for the viewer to feel before it's crushed.
**Fix:** Give the shore arrival its own breath: "You claw free and reach the coast. The sea has pulled back — bare rock where water stood. You are not looking at safety." This separates the escape beat from the tsunami beat, creating an actual turn the viewer feels.

### You-As-Subject — 7/10 (soft flag)

**Failing line (Clip 03):** "The sea is gone — debris from the eruption shoved it back. Then it returns."
**Problem:** At the catastrophe's peak, "you" vanishes for two consecutive sentences. The sea becomes the subject. The viewer shifts from participant to observer exactly when they should feel most trapped.
**Fix:** "You stare at bare seafloor. The water is coming back." Now "you" is present at the moment of realization, and the returning wave is framed as something bearing down on the viewer.
